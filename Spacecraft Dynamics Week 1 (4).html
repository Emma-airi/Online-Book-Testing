Vectors (Brief Recap)#

Prepared by: Hardit Saini, Emmanuel Airiofolo and Angadh Nanjangud

In this lecture we cover the following topics:

  1. Vector Operations

  2. Vector Operations: Cross Product and Related Formulas

  3. Time Derivative of a Vector

  4. content:Gravitational-Force-and-Newtons-Second-Law

  5. References

  • A vector is a quantity with a direction and a magnitude.

  • \(\vec{r}\), \(\underline{r}\), \(\boldsymbol{r}\) are all possible notations for vector quantities.

  • We describe a vector by its components:
    \(\vec{r}\) = \(r_x \hat{\imath}\) + \(r_y \hat{\jmath}\) + \(r_z \hat{k}\) = (\(r_x, r_y, r_z\))

    where \(\hat{\imath}\), \(\hat{\jmath}\), and \(\hat{k}\) are unit vectors aligned with a triad of orthogonal axes.

  • The magnitude is indicated by \(\|\mathbf{r}\|\) or simply by \(r\) and \begin{equation} r = \sqrt{r_x^2 + r_y^2 + r_z^2} \tag{1} \end{equation}

Vector Operations#

Multiplication of a Scalar with a Vector#

  • When a vector \(\mathbf{A}\) is multiplied by a scalar \(Q\), the result is a new vector \(Q\mathbf{A}\), which has the same direction as \(\mathbf{A}\) but a magnitude that is \(|Q|\) times the magnitude of \(\mathbf{A}\).


    \begin{equation} Q \mathbf{A} = Q (A_x \hat{\imath} + A_y \hat{\jmath} + A_z \hat{k}) = (Q A_x) \hat{\imath} + (Q A_y) \hat{\jmath} + (Q A_z) \hat{k} \tag{2} \end{equation}

Sum of Vectors#

  • The sum of two vectors \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) is a vector \(\mathbf{C}\) such that:
    \begin{equation} \mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} = (A_x + B_x) \hat{\imath} + (A_y + B_y) \hat{\jmath} + (A_z + B_z) \hat{k} \tag{3} \end{equation}

Parallelogram Rule#

  • The sum of two vectors can also be represented geometrically using the parallelogram rule:

    • Place the vectors \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) tail to tail.

    • Draw a parallelogram where \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) are adjacent sides.

    • The interception of the two projected lines represents the point where A is summed with B, \(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}\).

Dot Product Rule#

  • The dot product (or scalar product) of two vectors \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) is defined as:
    \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y + A_z B_z \tag{4} \end{equation}

    \begin{equation} or \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = |\mathbf{A}| |\mathbf{B}| \cos(\theta) \tag{5} \end{equation}
    where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\).

  • If one of the vectors is a unit vector (say, \(\hat{B}\)), the dot product gives the projection of \(\mathbf{A}\) onto \(\hat{B}\):
    \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \cdot \hat{B} = |\mathbf{A}| \cos(\theta) \end{equation}
    This is because \(|\hat{B}| = 1\).

Vector Operations: Cross Product and Related Formulas#

Cross Product#

  • The cross product of two vectors \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\), denoted \(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}\), results in a vector \(\mathbf{C}\) that is perpendicular to both \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\).

  • The magnitude of \(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}\) is given by: [ |\(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}|\) = \(|\mathbf{A}| |\mathbf{B}| \sin(\theta)\) ] where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\).

  • The direction of \(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}\) is determined by the right-hand rule.

(Cross, 2024)


\begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{\imath} & \hat{\jmath} & \hat{k} \ A_x & A_y & A_z \ B_x & B_y & B_z \end{vmatrix} = \left( A_y B_z - A_z B_y \right) \hat{\imath} - \left( A_x B_z - A_z B_x \right) \hat{\jmath} + \left( A_x B_y - A_y B_x \right) \hat{k} \tag{6} \end{equation}

Properties of the Cross Product#

  1. Anticommutative Property: \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = - (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{A}) \tag{7} \end{equation}

  2. Distributive Property: \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \times (\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} + \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{C} \tag{8} \end{equation}

  3. Scalar Multiplication: \begin{equation} (k \mathbf{A}) \times \mathbf{B} = k (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) = \mathbf{A} \times (k \mathbf{B}) \tag{9} \end{equation}

Triple Vector Product#

  • The triple vector product involves three vectors \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{C}\):


    \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \times (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{C}) \mathbf{B} - (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}) \mathbf{C} \tag{10} \end{equation} or \begin{equation} (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) \times \mathbf{C} = (\mathbf{C} \cdot \mathbf{B}) \mathbf{A} - (\mathbf{C} \cdot \mathbf{A}) \mathbf{B} \tag{11} \end{equation}

Scalar Triple Product#

  • The scalar triple product of three vectors \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{C}\) is a scalar quantity: \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \cdot (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) \tag{12} \end{equation}

  • This scalar represents the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the vectors \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{C}\).

Admin (2019)

  • It can be computed as:
    \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \cdot (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = \begin{vmatrix} A_x & A_y & A_z \ B_x & B_y & B_z \ C_x & C_y & C_z \end{vmatrix} \tag{13} \end{equation}

  • Interesting to note:
    \begin{equation} \mathbf{A} \cdot (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{B} \cdot (\mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{A}) = \mathbf{C} \cdot (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) \tag{14} \end{equation}

Time Derivative of a Vector#

  • The time derivative of a vector \(\mathbf{A}\) represents how the vector changes with respect to time.

In an Inertial Reference Frame#

  • The time derivative of a vector \(\mathbf{r}\) in an inertial reference frame is given by:


    \begin{equation} \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(x) \mathbf{i} + \frac{d}{dt}(y) \mathbf{j} + \frac{d}{dt}(z) \mathbf{k} \tag{15} \end{equation}

In a Rotating Reference Frame#

  • In a rotating reference frame with angular velocity \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\), the time derivative of the vector \(\mathbf{r}\) must account for the rotation:
    \begin{equation} \left( \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right){\text{inertial}} = \left( \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right){\text{rotating}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r} \tag{16} \end{equation}

    where:

    • \(\left( \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right)_{\text{rotating}}\) is the time derivative in the rotating frame

    • \(\left( \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right)_{\text{inertial}}\) is the time derivative in the inertial frame

    • \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\) is the angular velocity of the rotating frame

    • \(\times\) denotes the cross product (see above for more information)

    • This can also be expressed as:
      \begin{equation} \left( \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right){\text{rot}} = \left( \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right){\text{inertial}} - \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r} \tag{17} \end{equation}

Gravitational Force and Newton’s Second Law#

  • The gravitational force between two masses \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) is given by:

Gravitational Force#

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{F}{12} &= \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2} \hat{r} = \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^3} \mathbf{r} \ \mathbf{F}{21} &= -\frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2} \hat{r} = -\frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^3} \mathbf{r} \end{aligned} \tag{18} \end{equation}

where:

  • \(G = 6.672 \times 10^{-11} \ \text{kg}^{-1} \ \text{m}^3 \ \text{s}^{-2}\) (Universal gravitational constant)

  • \(\hat{r} = \frac{\mathbf{r}}{|\mathbf{r}|}\) is the unit vector

  • \(\mathbf{F}_{12}\) represents the force on \(m_1\) exerted by \(m_2\)’s gravitational attraction.

  • \(\mathbf{F}_{21}\) represents the force on \(m_2\) exerted by \(m_1\)’s gravitational attraction.

Center of Mass ( G )#

The center of mass \(\mathbf{r}_G\) of the system is given by:

\begin{equation} \mathbf{r}_G = \frac{m_1 \mathbf{r}_1 + m_2 \mathbf{r}_2}{m_1 + m_2} \tag{19} \end{equation}

Newton’s Second Law (F = ma)#

  1. \begin{equation} m_1 \ddot{\mathbf{r}}1 = \mathbf{F}{12} \tag{20} \end{equation}

  2. \begin{equation} m_2 \ddot{\mathbf{r}}2 = \mathbf{F}{21} \tag{21} \end{equation}

Substituting equations (18) into equations (20) + (21):

\begin{equation} m_1 \ddot{\mathbf{r}}1 + m_2 \ddot{\mathbf{r}}2 = \mathbf{F}{12} + \mathbf{F}{21} = \frac{G m_1 m_2 \mathbf{r}}{r^3} - \frac{G m_1 m_2 \mathbf{r}}{r^3} = 0 \tag{22} \end{equation}

Thus:

\begin{equation} \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_G = 0, \quad \dot{\mathbf{r}}_G = \text{constant} \tag{23} \end{equation}

This implies that \(G\) can be chosen as the origin of the inertial frame.

Two-Body Relative Dynamics#

Newton’s Second Law#

  1. \begin{equation} m_2 \ddot{\mathbf{r}}2 = \mathbf{F}{21} = - \frac{G m_1 m_2 \mathbf{r}}{r^3} \rightarrow \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_2 = - \frac{G m_1 \mathbf{r}}{r^3} \tag{24} \end{equation}

  2. \begin{equation} m_1 \ddot{\mathbf{r}}1 = \mathbf{F}{12} = \frac{G m_1 m_2 \mathbf{r}}{r^3} \rightarrow \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_1 = \frac{G m_2 \mathbf{r}}{r^3} \tag{25} \end{equation}

Equation (24) - (25):

\begin{equation} \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_2 - \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_1 = - \frac{G m_1 \mathbf{r}}{r^3} - \frac{G m_2 \mathbf{r}}{r^3} = - \frac{G (m_1 + m_2) \mathbf{r}}{r^3} \tag{26} \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = - \frac{G (m_1 + m_2)}{r^3} \mathbf{r} \tag{27} \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = - \frac{\mu \mathbf{r}}{r^3} \tag{28} \end{equation}

where \(\mu\) is the gravitational parameter.

Implications of the Equation of Motion#

  • If \(m_1 \gg m_2\)

    e.g., \(m_1\) is \(m_\oplus = 5.974 \times 10^{24} \ \text{kg}\) and \(m_2\) is a spacecraft, \(m_2 \approx 1000 \ \text{kg}\)

\begin{equation} \mathbf{r}_G \approx \mathbf{r}_1 \tag{29} \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \mu \approx G m_1 \tag{30} \end{equation}

This is referred to as a restricted two-body problem.
