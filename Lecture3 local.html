The Eccentricity Vector#

Deriving The Eccentricity Vector#

The Eccentricity vector \(\vec{e}\) helps us describe the shape of an orbit and its direction. We can find it by taking the cross product of the velocity and the specific angular momentum:

  1. Such as:

    \[ \ddot{\vec{r}} = - \frac{\mu}{r^3} \vec{r} \]

    Taking the cross product with the specific angular momentum, \(\vec{h}\):

    \[ \ddot{\vec{r}}\times\vec{h} = \textcolor{red}{- \frac{\mu}{r^3} \vec{r}}\times\vec{h} \]

    Using the rule for \(\vec{a}\times\vec{b}\times{c}=(\vec{c}\cdot\vec{a})\vec{b}-(\vec{b}\cdot\vec{a})\vec{c}\) with \(\vec{h}=\vec{r}\cdot\dot{\vec{r}}\): $\( -\frac{\mu}{r^3}[(\dot{\vec{r}}\cdot\vec{r})\vec{r}-(\vec{r}\cdot\vec{r})\dot{\vec{r}}]\rightarrow-\frac{\mu}{r^3}(\dot{r}r\vec{r}-r^2\dot{\vec{r}})\rightarrow-\frac{\mu}{r^2}(\dot{r}\vec{r}-r\dot{\vec{r}})\\\Rightarrow =\frac{d}{dt}(\mu\frac{\vec{r}}{r}) \)$ This gives us:

    \[ \Rightarrow \dot{\vec{r}}\times\vec{h} - \mu\frac{\vec{r}}{r} = constant \]

    Which can be rewritten as: $\( \bf{\vec{e}=\frac{\dot{\vec{r}}\times\vec{h}}{\mu}-\frac{\vec{r}}{r}} \)$ This is our equation for the eccentricity vector!

  2. The first integrals of motion:

    • \(\vec{e}\) is a constant vector, implying both constant direction and magnitude.

    • \(\vec{e}\) lies in the plane of motion and is fixed.

    Let’s define:

    \[ \textbf{e} = \frac{\vec{e}}{\mu} \]

    where \(e\) is the eccentricity.

Properties#

  • \(\vec{e}\) lies in the plane of motion and (since it is constant) has a fixed magnitude and direction. \(\Rightarrow \hat{i}\equiv\vec{e}\), so we can use the eccentricity as one of the axes.

  • The eccentricity, \(e=|\vec{e}| \geq 0\), determines the shape of the orbit:

    • \(e = 0\): Circular orbit

    • \(0 < e < 1\): Elliptic orbit

    • \(e = 1\): Parabolic orbit

    • \(e > 1\): Hyperbolic orbit

The Equation of the Orbit#

If we look at the dot product of \(\vec{e}\cdot\vec{r}\) we know that: \(\vec{r}\cdot\vec{e}=recos\theta\) and if we recall \(\vec{a}\cdot(\vec{b}\times\vec{c})=(\vec{a}\times\vec{b})\cdot\vec{c}\) then: $\( \frac{\vec{r}\cdot(\dot{\vec{r}}\times\dot{\vec{h}})}{\mu}-\frac{\vec{r}\cdot\vec{r}}{r}=\frac{(\vec{r}\times\dot{\vec{r}})\cdot\vec{h}}{\mu} - \frac{r^2}{r} = \frac{h^2}{\mu}-r \)\( So then this equivalence implies: \)\( r = \frac{\frac{h^2}{\mu}}{1+ecos\theta} \)$

\(\theta\) is called the true anomaly.

This is another way of expressing Kepler’s 1st Law - the orbit of a planet is an ellipse with the sun at one of the two focii. This is an Equation of the Orbit.

Note: \(\theta\) can also be expressed as \(v\) or \(f\)

r describes the shape of the orbits as conic sections through the varying eccentricities shown before but it is important to note that:

  • Operative orbits are always circular or elliptic. As these keep the satellite closest to the planet of interest for as long as possible.

  • Hyperbolic orbits are maneuvers used on interplanetary missions to reduce propellant use by using the gravity of a body to reach another target body.

Elliptic Orbits#

a = semi-major axis
b = semi-minor axis
p = semi-latus rectum
\(\textcolor{#646464}{\vec{e}}\) is aligned with the apsides and point towards the perigee

Key Parameters#

  1. semi-major axis \(a\): This is the axis of the ellipse that has the longest diameter.

  2. semi-minor axis \(b\): This is the axis of the ellipse with the shortest diameter.

  3. Semi-latus rectum \(p\): This is the distance from the foci (orbitted body) to the orbit measured perpendicular to the semi-major axis.

    \[ p = a(1 - e^2) \]

  4. Perigee (\(r_p\)): The lowest point in an orbit/the point closest to the orbitted body. $\( r_p = a(1 - e) \)$

  5. Apogee (\(r_a\)): The highest point in an orbit/the point furthest from the orbitted body. $\( r_a = a(1 + e) \)$

Determination of the Orbit#

  1. Given focus \(\vec{r_o}\) and \(\vec{v_o}\), compute \(\vec{h}\) and \(\vec{e}\).

  2. Compute the semi-latus rectum, \(p = \frac{h^2}{\mu}\)

  3. Compute the semi-major axis,\(a\), using \(p\): \(a=\frac{p}{1-e^2}\)

  4. Compute the apsides points (most extreme points in the orbit): \(r_p = a(1-e), r_a = a(1+e)\)

Vis-Viva Equation#

The Vis-Viva Equation is used to find the velocity at any given r when a maneuver is planned.

The Equation:
The total energy of the orbit is the sum of the kinetic energy and the potential energies of the orbit. $\( E= \frac 1 2 v^2-\frac{\mu}{r} = \frac 1 2 \dot{r^2} + \frac 1 2 \frac{h^2}{r^2} - \frac{\mu}{r} = \frac 1 2 \dot{r^2} + \phi_{eff} \)\( The sum of the potential energies is the effective potential energy, \)\phi_{eff}\(: \)\( \phi_{eff} = \frac 1 2 \frac{h^2}{r^2}-\frac{\mu}{r} \)$

At perigee and apogee the velocity = 0 so \(E_{r_p}=\phi_{eff}(r_p)\) so we can derive an equation for the specific orbital energy as a function of \(a\). $\( E=-\frac{\mu}{2a} \)$

\(\Rightarrow\) orbits with the same semi-major axis have the same orbital energy.

This means that the Vis-Viva Equation is: $\( E = \frac 1 2 v^2 - \frac{\mu}{r}= -\frac{\mu}{2a} \)$

So the velocity of an orbitting body can be found whether you have the position or the semi-major axis of its orbit.

Conclusion#

  • The eccentricity vector is a fundamental constant vector in orbital mechanics.

  • The orbit equation describes different conic sections based on the value of the eccentricity \(e\).

  • The Vis Viva equation is a crucial tool in mission planning for calculating velocities at various points in an orbit.
